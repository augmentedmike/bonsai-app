## Topic: The Specific Detail Test — Why Vague Copy Kills Conversion

The most common copywriting failure isn't bad grammar or weak headlines — it's vagueness. "Powerful," "seamless," "next-level," "innovative" — these words appear on millions of landing pages and mean nothing to the reader. The specific detail test is a discipline that forces you to replace vague claims with concrete, credible specifics, and it consistently improves conversion rates more than almost any other copy intervention.

**Why vague copy fails**

When readers encounter vague marketing language, one of two things happens: they either skip past it (pattern-matching it as boilerplate) or they become skeptical (everyone says "powerful," so that claim has zero signal). Either outcome is bad. Vague copy wastes your most precious real estate — the moments when someone is actually reading your words.

Specific copy, by contrast, does something different: it creates a *mental image*. Mental images are sticky, credible, and memorable. "We help teams move faster" is vague. "Teams using our tool cut sprint planning from 4 hours to 45 minutes" is specific — you can picture it, you can evaluate its plausibility, and you can decide if it applies to you.

**The test itself**

Take any piece of copy you've written. For every adjective and abstract claim, ask: *Can I replace this with a number, a name, a specific action, or a concrete result?*

| Vague | Specific |
|---|---|
| "Trusted by thousands of customers" | "Used by 12,400 teams across 38 countries" |
| "Dramatically faster deployment" | "Deploy in 4 minutes, down from 2 hours" |
| "Easy to use" | "Set up in under 10 minutes, no IT required" |
| "Comprehensive analytics" | "Track 47 metrics across every step of your funnel" |
| "We understand your challenges" | "We've talked to 200+ [role] at [company size] companies" |

Not every claim needs a number — sometimes a specific *action* or *name* does more work: "Used by the marketing teams at Notion, Linear, and Figma" beats "used by fast-growing SaaS companies."

**Where to apply this first**

Priority order for maximum leverage:
1. **Hero headline and subheadline** — the highest-traffic copy real estate
2. **Feature descriptions** — translate features into specific outcomes
3. **Social proof** — replace "our customers love us" with a specific quoted result
4. **CTA buttons** — "Get started" is vague; "Start your free 14-day trial" is specific

**The AI writing context**

AI writing assistants default to vague because they're trained on the entire internet, which is full of vague marketing copy. When using AI to draft copy, your editing job is primarily to run the specific detail test on everything it produces. Add your real numbers, your real customer names, your real before-and-after outcomes. AI drafts the shape; you fill in the substance.

**One more principle: specificity implies expertise**

Specific copy signals that you *know your subject deeply*. Vague copy signals that you're describing something you don't fully understand — or don't believe in. Readers feel this intuitively even if they can't name it. Specificity builds trust without explicitly claiming to be trustworthy.

Run the test on your next piece. Replace three vague phrases with concrete specifics. Notice how the copy immediately sounds more credible — and more worth reading.

## Key Takeaway
Replace vague adjectives and abstract claims with specific numbers, names, and concrete outcomes — specificity is the single highest-leverage copywriting edit because it converts skeptical readers into believers.
